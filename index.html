1Code of Ethics — The Five Values

All 12 Standards flow from five core values. They're always described as overlapping, not separate boxes, because in real scenarios they pull against each other — good ethical decision-making is about weighing them together, not ticking one off at a time.

  • Trustworthiness — clients can rely on you to act in their interests, even when no one's checking.
  • Competence — you maintain the knowledge and skill to give advice that's actually fit for purpose.
  • Honesty — you don't mislead, exaggerate, or omit material facts.
  • Fairness — you don't let bias, product ties, or personal gain distort the advice.
  • Diligence — you do the work properly: research, follow-through, timeliness.
Worked Example
A client asks you to recommend a fund that pays your practice higher trail commission but isn't clearly the best option available. Which values are in tension, and which should prevail under the Code?
Fairness and Trustworthiness are compromised by self-interest. The Code requires the client's best interests to prevail — disclose the conflict and recommend based on suitability, not remuneration (ties to Standard 3: conflicts of interest).

2Overlapping Values — The Elimination Technique

This is one of the most common ways FASEA tries to catch people out: "it's not this value — what else could it be?" It's testing whether you understand the values individually, not whether you can recite the list. Each value answers a different question — when one is ruled out, work out which question actually fits the scenario.

ValueThe question it answers
HonestyWas the information true, complete, not misleading?
FairnessDid self-interest, bias, or a conflict distort the outcome?
CompetenceDid the adviser have and apply the right knowledge and skill?
DiligenceWas the work actually followed through with proper care?
TrustworthinessCan this person be relied on to do the right thing, consistently, even unsupervised?

Worked example — "it's not Honesty, what else could it be?"

Scenario: An adviser recommends Fund A over the objectively better Fund B because Fund A pays higher trail commission. The adviser doesn't lie about anything — they just don't mention the commission difference.

  • Ask: was anything untrue? No — so it's not Honesty.
  • Ask: did self-interest distort the recommendation? Yes — that's Fairness.

Other common swaps to drill

  • "Not Competence, what else?" — Adviser clearly knows the right answer but rushed or skipped steps → Diligence, not Competence. Competence is about capability; Diligence is about effort and thoroughness.
  • "Not Honesty, what else?" — Adviser broke a promise (said they'd call back, didn't) but never said anything false → Trustworthiness, not Honesty. Honesty is about words; Trustworthiness is about reliability of conduct over time.
  • "Not Fairness, what else?" — No conflict of interest at all, but the adviser genuinely didn't understand the product → Competence, not Fairness.
The habit to build: every time you eliminate a value, name specifically what failed — a fact, a bias, a skill gap, a follow-through gap, or a broken commitment. Whichever of those five things it was, that tells you the value — each value only "owns" one of them.

3Deontological vs Consequentialist Ethics

Deontological ethics = duty-based. An action is right or wrong based on rules/duties, regardless of outcome. The Code of Ethics is fundamentally deontological — you follow the standards because they're your duty, not because a particular client outcome worked out well.

Consequentialist ethics = outcome-based. An action is judged by its results (e.g. "the client ended up better off, so it was fine").

Exam pattern: a scenario shows a good outcome reached through a questionable process. The correct FASEA answer is almost always the deontological one — the duty was breached, regardless of outcome.

4Values & Standards — Quick Structure

5 Values underpin 12 Standards (the specific, testable rules), including:

  • Standard 1 — Act in accordance with all applicable laws
  • Standard 2 — Act with honesty and integrity, and in the best interests of clients
  • Standard 3 — Not advise, refer, or act where you have a conflict of interest or duty
  • Standard 5 — All advice and product recommendations must be offered in good faith, with competence, and in the client's best interests
  • Standard 6 — Consider the broader, long-term interests and likely circumstances of the client
  • Standard 12 — Uphold and promote the ethical standards of the profession

You don't need every standard word-for-word — you need to be able to identify which standard a scenario breaches.

5DDO, ASIC RGs & AML/CTF

Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO)

Product issuers must make a Target Market Determination (TMD) for each product. Distributors (including advisers) must take reasonable steps to ensure the product is distributed consistently with that TMD. Recommending outside it needs to be justified and documented.

ASIC Regulatory Guides (RGs)

ASIC's RGs explain how ASIC interprets and administers the law — guidance, not law itself. Key ones: RG 175 (licensing, conduct & disclosure), RG 244 (general/scaled advice), RG 246 (conflicted remuneration).

AML/CTF

Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006. Practical relevance for advisers: client identification (KYC) before providing designated services, plus ongoing monitoring for suspicious activity.

Worked Example
A client wants to invest via a company structure with an overseas beneficial owner who won't provide ID. What's the compliance issue?
AML/CTF customer identification requirements aren't met — you can't proceed with the designated service until beneficial ownership is verified.

8Wholesale vs Retail Clients

A client is wholesale (fewer disclosure protections) if they meet any of:

  • Net assets test — net assets ≥ $2.5 million
  • Income test — gross income ≥ $250,000 in each of the last two financial years, plus a qualified accountant's certificate
  • Product value test — the financial product/service is ≥ $500,000
  • Professional / sophisticated investor tests
  • Business/investment company tests

Everyone else defaults to retail, with the full suite of protections.

10Factual, General & Personal Advice

  • Factual information — objective facts only, no opinion or recommendation (e.g. "the cash rate is 4.35%"). No advice obligations attach.
  • General advice — doesn't consider the client's objectives, financial situation or needs. Must carry a general advice warning.
  • Personal advice — considers (or a reasonable person would expect it to consider) the client's objectives, financial situation, or needs. Triggers the best interests duty and requires an SOA/ROA.
Worked Example

Likely personal advice — referencing the audience's circumstances ("near retirement") means a reasonable person could expect their personal situation was considered, even without 1:1 interaction.

11Who Can Advise on What: Mortgages, Insurance & Credit

This hinges on the definition of "relevant financial product" — it determines who needs to be a FASEA-qualified relevant provider.

Relevant financial productsLife insurance, super, SMSF, investments

Must be advised on by a relevant provider: AFSL holder or authorised representative, listed on ASIC's Financial Advisers Register, FASEA-qualified (degree, exam, Code of Ethics, CPD). This is the adviser role you and Nathan are training for.

Excluded productGeneral insurance — car, home, contents, travel

General insurance is specifically excluded from the "relevant financial product" definition, so it doesn't trigger the Best Interests Duty, SOA, or FASEA education/exam requirements. It's given by insurance brokers or insurer staff under an AFSL, usually as general advice, requiring only Tier 1 or Tier 2 (RG146) training rather than full adviser qualifications.

Different Act entirelyMortgages & car loans (credit products)

Regulated by the National Consumer Credit Protection Act 2009 (NCCP), not the Corporations Act. Requires an Australian Credit Licence (ACL), or being a credit representative of one — this is mortgage/finance broker territory, not financial adviser territory. Since 2021, mortgage brokers have their own separate Best Interests Duty. A financial adviser can't recommend a specific loan/mortgage product unless they also hold credit authorisation.

Worked Example
Nathan gives a client general advice on comprehensive car insurance and mentions a good mortgage broker for their home loan. Does he need to be a relevant provider for either?
No to both — general insurance is excluded from "relevant financial product," and referring to a mortgage broker (rather than advising on the loan terms himself) doesn't require a credit licence. If he wanted to advise on the loan terms himself, he'd need ACL authorisation.
