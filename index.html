1Code of Ethics — The Five Values

All 12 Standards flow from five core values. They're always described as overlapping, not separate boxes, because in real scenarios they pull against each other — good ethical decision-making is about weighing them together, not ticking one off at a time.

  • Trustworthiness — clients can rely on you to act in their interests, even when no one's checking.
  • Competence — you maintain the knowledge and skill to give advice that's actually fit for purpose.
  • Honesty — you don't mislead, exaggerate, or omit material facts.
  • Fairness — you don't let bias, product ties, or personal gain distort the advice.
  • Diligence — you do the work properly: research, follow-through, timeliness.
Worked Example
A client asks you to recommend a fund that pays your practice higher trail commission but isn't clearly the best option available. Which values are in tension, and which should prevail under the Code?
Fairness and Trustworthiness are compromised by self-interest. The Code requires the client's best interests to prevail — disclose the conflict and recommend based on suitability, not remuneration (ties to Standard 3: conflicts of interest).

2Overlapping Values — The Elimination Technique

This is one of the most common ways FASEA tries to catch people out: "it's not this value — what else could it be?" It's testing whether you understand the values individually, not whether you can recite the list. Each value answers a different question — when one is ruled out, work out which question actually fits the scenario.

ValueThe question it answers
HonestyWas the information true, complete, not misleading?
FairnessDid self-interest, bias, or a conflict distort the outcome?
CompetenceDid the adviser have and apply the right knowledge and skill?
DiligenceWas the work actually followed through with proper care?
TrustworthinessCan this person be relied on to do the right thing, consistently, even unsupervised?

Worked Example — "It's Not Honesty, What Else Could It Be?"

Scenario
An adviser recommends Fund A over the objectively better Fund B because Fund A pays higher trail commission. The adviser doesn't lie about anything — they just don't mention the commission difference.
Ask: was anything untrue? No — so it's not Honesty. Ask: did self-interest distort the recommendation? Yes — that's Fairness.

Other common swaps to drill

  • "Not Competence, what else?" — Adviser clearly knows the right answer but rushed or skipped steps → Diligence, not Competence. Competence is about capability; Diligence is about effort and thoroughness.
  • "Not Honesty, what else?" — Adviser broke a promise (said they'd call back, didn't) but never said anything false → Trustworthiness, not Honesty. Honesty is about words; Trustworthiness is about reliability of conduct over time.
  • "Not Fairness, what else?" — No conflict of interest at all, but the adviser genuinely didn't understand the product → Competence, not Fairness.
The habit to build: every time you eliminate a value, name specifically what failed — a fact, a bias, a skill gap, a follow-through gap, or a broken commitment. Whichever of those five things it was, that tells you the value — each value only "owns" one of them.

More Practice Scenarios

Six more swaps, each isolating a different pair of values:

  • Not Diligence, what else? — Adviser ticks every checklist box and submits on time, but recommends a geared investment without understanding how gearing amplifies losses → Competence. The process was followed perfectly; the gap is knowledge, not effort.
  • Not Fairness, what else? — Adviser fully discloses a conflict of interest and the client is happy to proceed, but the adviser then misses the compliance lodgement deadline → Diligence. The conflict itself was handled properly; what failed was follow-through.

3Deontological vs Consequentialist Ethics

Deontological ethics = duty-based. An action is right or wrong based on rules/duties, regardless of outcome. The Code of Ethics is fundamentally deontological — you follow the standards because they're your duty, not because a particular client outcome worked out well.

Consequentialist ethics = outcome-based. An action is judged by its results (e.g. "the client ended up better off, so it was fine").

Exam pattern: a scenario shows a good outcome reached through a questionable process. The correct FASEA answer is almost always the deontological one — the duty was breached, regardless of outcome.
Worked Example
A client is unhappy to learn their adviser never completed a full needs analysis before recommending a life insurance policy — even though the policy turned out to suit them well. The client asks: "It worked out fine, so what's the problem?" How would you explain the issue?
Under a deontological view, the duty to complete a proper needs analysis existed regardless of the outcome. The adviser still breached that duty (touching Standards 5 and 6) — a good result doesn't retroactively justify skipping the process. This is separate from whether the client actually suffered a loss.

4Values & Standards — Quick Structure

5 Values underpin 12 Standards (the specific, testable rules), including:

  • Standard 1 — Act in accordance with all applicable laws
  • Standard 2 — Act with honesty and integrity, and in the best interests of clients
  • Standard 3 — Not advise, refer, or act where you have a conflict of interest or duty
  • Standard 5 — All advice and product recommendations must be offered in good faith, with competence, and in the client's best interests
  • Standard 6 — Consider the broader, long-term interests and likely circumstances of the client
  • Standard 12 — Uphold and promote the ethical standards of the profession

You don't need every standard word-for-word — you need to be able to identify which standard a scenario breaches.

Worked Example
A client later discovers their adviser recommended a fund managed by the adviser's own related entity, without ever mentioning the connection. Which Standard does this most directly breach?
Standard 3 — acting where there's an undisclosed conflict of interest or duty. It may also touch Standard 2 (honesty and best interests) and Standard 5, but the conflict itself is squarely a Standard 3 issue.

5DDO, ASIC RGs & AML/CTF

Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO)

Product issuers must make a Target Market Determination (TMD) for each product. Distributors (including advisers) must take reasonable steps to ensure the product is distributed consistently with that TMD. Recommending outside it needs to be justified and documented.

What's Actually In a TMD

  • Target market — the class of consumers the product is likely to be appropriate for, based on their objectives, financial situation and needs.
  • Distribution conditions — any conditions or restrictions on how it can be distributed (e.g. only via personal advice, not direct to consumers).
  • Review triggers — events that require the issuer to review the TMD (e.g. a significant dealing outside the target market, a spike in complaints, a material product change).
  • Review periods — the maximum time allowed between scheduled reviews of the TMD.

Distributor Obligations

  • Take reasonable steps to distribute the product consistently with its TMD.
  • Notify the issuer of a significant dealing outside the target market within 10 business days.
  • Keep records of distribution conduct and complaints, and report this data to the issuer as required.
Worked Example
An adviser recommends a high-growth geared fund (TMD target market: experienced investors with high risk tolerance) to a conservative retiree client, because it's the only product that satisfies a specific tax objective. What's the DDO issue?
This is a significant dealing outside the TMD. The adviser needs to justify and document why it's still appropriate for this specific client (consistent with the best interests duty), and the distributor should notify the issuer of the significant dealing within 10 business days.

AML/CTF

Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006. What it actually requires of an adviser:

  • Customer identification (KYC) — verify who the client is before providing a designated service.
  • Ongoing customer due diligence — monitor transactions and check they're consistent with what you know about the client.
  • Enhanced due diligence — extra scrutiny for higher-risk clients, e.g. politically exposed persons (PEPs) or those connected to high-risk jurisdictions.
  • Reporting to AUSTRAC — Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs) for suspicious activity, Threshold Transaction Reports (TTRs) for cash-equivalent transactions of $10,000 or more.
  • Record-keeping — retain identification and transaction records for at least 7 years.

AML/CTF Red Flags

  • Reluctance or refusal to provide identification documents.
  • Complex ownership structures with no clear commercial rationale.
  • Transactions inconsistent with the client's known profile or stated purpose.
  • Unusual urgency or pressure to bypass normal processes.
  • Large cash movements or third-party funding with no clear relationship to the client.
Worked Example
A client wants to invest via a company structure with an overseas beneficial owner who won't provide ID. What's the compliance issue?
AML/CTF customer identification requirements aren't met — you can't proceed with the designated service until beneficial ownership is verified.
Worked Example
A new client wants to urgently transfer $50,000 into an investment account from an unrelated third party, and is reluctant to explain the relationship. What should the adviser do?
This is a red flag — unclear third-party funding plus urgency. Pause, apply enhanced due diligence, and consider whether a Suspicious Matter Report to AUSTRAC is warranted, rather than proceeding under normal timeframes.

9SMSF & Insurance

  • Advising on establishing an SMSF is personal advice requiring a full best-interests process, with extra obligations since 2021 (e.g. comparing against an APRA-regulated fund).
  • Insurance inside super — consider premium funding via contributions, impact on retirement balance, and whether cover type (e.g. Total and Permanent Disability, TPD, "any occupation" vs "own occupation") suits the member.
  • SMSF trustees carry personal liability — needs to be explained as part of suitability.

Trustee Structures

  • Individual trustees — each member is a trustee (minimum two, unless it's a single-member fund with special rules). Assets must be held in all trustees' names, which gets messy on death, incapacity, or a change of membership.
  • Corporate trustee — a company acts as trustee, with each member as a director. Cleaner separation between personal and fund assets, smoother succession, easier for single-member funds — but carries ASIC company fees.

Contributions

  • Concessional (before-tax) — employer SG, salary sacrifice, and personal deductible contributions combined. General cap: $32,500 for FY2026–27 (indexed annually — always confirm the current figure). Unused cap from up to 5 previous years can be carried forward if total super balance is under $500,000.
  • Non-concessional (after-tax) — general cap: $130,000 for FY2026–27 (4× the concessional cap). The bring-forward rule can allow up to 3 years' worth (up to $390,000) in a single year, subject to eligibility and total super balance thresholds.
  • Non-concessional cap drops to nil if the member's total super balance is at or above the general transfer balance cap ($2.1 million from FY2026–27).

Pension Phase

  • Accumulation phase — earnings taxed at up to 15%.
  • Retirement (pension) phase — earnings on assets supporting an account-based pension are generally tax-free, up to the member's transfer balance cap.
  • Transfer balance cap — limits how much can be moved into the tax-free retirement phase ($2.1 million from FY2026–27).
  • Minimum pension drawdown — an age-based percentage of the pension balance must be drawn each year once in pension phase.

Compliance Basics

  • Sole purpose test — the fund must be maintained solely to provide retirement benefits to members (or their dependants on death).
  • In-house asset rule — investments in related parties are generally capped at 5% of the fund's total assets.
  • Arm's length dealing — all fund transactions must be on commercial, arm's length terms.
  • Investment strategy — must be documented, addressing diversification, liquidity, risk/return, and insurance for members.
  • Annual audit — every SMSF must be audited each year by an approved SMSF auditor, and lodge an annual return.
Figures above are indicative for FY2026–27 and are indexed periodically — always check the current year's caps before using them with a client.
Worked Example
A 45-year-old client has a total super balance of $450,000 and wants to contribute $300,000 from a recent inheritance into their SMSF this financial year. Can they do this?
Potentially yes, using the non-concessional bring-forward rule. Their total super balance is well under the $2.1 million transfer balance cap, so they're likely eligible to bring forward up to three years' worth of the non-concessional cap (up to $390,000) in one year — meaning the $300,000 contribution could fit within that bring-forward amount, rather than being limited to the standard annual $130,000 cap.
Worked Example
An SMSF trustee wants the fund to buy a holiday house that the trustee's family can use on weekends. Is this allowed?
No. This breaches the sole purpose test — the fund's assets must be maintained solely to provide retirement benefits, not personal enjoyment for members or their families. It would likely also breach the in-house asset and arm's length dealing rules if a related party gets personal use of the asset.

This hinges on the definition of "relevant financial product" — it determines who needs to be a FASEA-qualified relevant provider.

Excluded productGeneral insurance — car, home, contents, travel

Different Act entirelyMortgages & car loans (credit products)

Worked Example

12Breaches — Who's Responsible

When something goes wrong, the exam wants you to identify where the actual failure occurred — that's what determines who's responsible, not just who's easiest to blame.

The Three Layers

  • The individual adviser — personally responsible for the advice they give: following the best interests duty, complying with the Code of Ethics, and following their licensee's compliance procedures on each file.
  • The licensee (AFSL holder) — responsible under s912A of the Corporations Act for having adequate arrangements to supervise and monitor its representatives, provide training, and maintain competent compliance systems. A licensee can be liable even when it wasn't the licensee who gave the bad advice.
  • The product issuer — responsible for the accuracy of the PDS, the TMD, and the product's design. If a problem stems from a flawed disclosure document or product design, liability generally sits with the issuer — not the adviser who reasonably relied on it.
Worked Example 1
An adviser doesn't complete a proper fact find and recommends unsuitable insurance to a client. Who's responsible?
Primarily the individual adviser — this is a personal best interests duty and Code of Ethics breach. The licensee may also carry some responsibility if its training or supervision was inadequate, and must assess the matter as a possible reportable situation.
Worked Example 2
A product's PDS understates its fees due to a calculation error made by the issuer. An adviser reasonably relies on the PDS and recommends the product. Who's responsible?
The product issuer — the error originates in their disclosure document. The adviser and licensee generally aren't liable if they reasonably relied on the PDS, unless they knew, or should have known, that it was wrong.
Worked Example 3
A licensee's compliance system fails to flag a pattern of unsuitable advice happening across several advisers. Who's responsible?
The licensee — this is a systemic breach of its s912A supervisory obligations, even though the individual advisers involved have also breached their own duties. Both can be responsible at once, for different reasons.
Quick test: ask "did this fail at the point of advice (adviser), at the point of supervision (licensee), or at the point of product/disclosure design (issuer)?" — responsibility can land on more than one layer at once, but each layer is judged on its own failure.

14Conflicted Remuneration & Soft Dollar Benefits

What Counts as Conflicted Remuneration

Under s963A of the Corporations Act, conflicted remuneration is any benefit given to a licensee or representative that could reasonably be expected to influence the advice given, or the products recommended.

Generally Banned

  • Commissions on investment and superannuation products.
  • Volume-based payments or bonuses tied to sales targets or the volume of business placed with a particular product provider ("shelf-space" fees).
  • Soft dollar benefits above the minimal threshold, especially if tied to volume or conditional on recommending particular products.

Generally Permitted

  • General insurance commissions.
  • Life risk insurance commissions, capped under the Life Insurance Framework (LIF).
  • Fee for service paid directly by the client — not conflicted, since it doesn't depend on which product is recommended.
  • Non-monetary benefits below the minimal threshold that don't influence advice (e.g. genuinely educational training).

Four Types of "Rewards" Worth Distinguishing

  • Soft dollar benefits — non-cash perks like conference attendance, subsidised training, entertainment, or gifts from product providers. Must be recorded in a soft dollar benefits register once above a minimal threshold, and must not be tied to volume or influence advice.
  • Volume-based (shelf-space) rewards — payments from a product provider to a licensee based on total funds under management or sales volume placed with that provider. Generally banned, since it directly rewards steering business toward one provider.
  • Rewards from the licensee itself — internal bonuses, KPIs, or promotion tied to selling in-house or proprietary products. This is a vertical integration risk (where the same corporate group owns both the advice business and the products being recommended) flagged repeatedly by the 2018–19 Royal Commission into misconduct in the banking and financial services industry — can be conflicted remuneration if not carefully managed.
  • Rewards from product or ETF providers — fund managers or ETF issuers offering research trips, sponsorships, or co-branded marketing funding. Only permissible if genuinely non-conflicted: education-focused, not tied to sales, below thresholds, and properly disclosed.
Worked Example
A fund manager pays a licensee a bonus calculated as a percentage of total client funds placed into its products across the year. Is this conflicted remuneration?
Yes — this is a volume-based/shelf-space payment. It directly rewards the licensee for steering more business toward that provider, regardless of whether it's actually the best option for each client, and is generally banned.

Ethical Discussion Questions

  • If a soft dollar benefit sits below the disclosure threshold, does that make it ethically fine — or just legally exempt from the register requirement?
  • Can an adviser genuinely stay objective after accepting hospitality from a product provider, even if they believe they can?
  • Would disclosure alone be enough to manage a volume-based reward, or does the conflict need to be removed entirely?
  • How should a licensee's internal reward structure (KPIs, bonuses) be designed so it doesn't quietly steer advisers toward proprietary products?
  • Where's the line between legitimate professional development and a disguised inducement?

15Left Field Bullshit Questions

Every FASEA-style exam has a handful of questions that aren't really testing whether you know the content — they're testing whether you can read carefully under pressure. Same knowledge, deliberately deployed to trip you up. Here's the taxonomy of tricks, then ten questions built to demonstrate each one.

The Trick Taxonomy

  • The "EXCEPT" flip — "All of the following are X, EXCEPT" makes you hunt for the odd one out in the opposite direction to normal. Easy to rush and pick a right-sounding wrong answer out of habit.
  • The red herring scenario — extra scenario detail (day of the week, appointment length, unrelated small talk) that has zero bearing on the answer, included purely to see if you can filter signal from noise.
  • The double negative — "Which of the following would NOT be considered inconsistent with..." Your brain often skips the "NOT" and answers the opposite question.
  • The "disclosure fixes everything" trap — disclosure is necessary in many contexts, but for banned conflicted remuneration, disclosure doesn't cure it. Don't assume disclosure is always the answer.
  • The "MOST correct" trap — two or three options are technically defensible, but one is clearly the primary, most direct fit. FASEA wants the best answer, not just a true one.
  • The absolute word trap — "always," "never," "guarantees," "only." Real regulatory frameworks are full of exceptions; an option using absolute language is very often the wrong one.
  • The close-number trap — wrong answers placed suspiciously near the real figure (5 vs 10 vs 14 business days). Know your exact numbers rather than "vibing" a close one.
  • The "nothing's actually wrong here" trap — some scenarios describe someone doing everything correctly. The question primes you to hunt for a breach that doesn't exist. Sometimes the right answer is that nothing went wrong.
  • The same-name, different-regime trap — "best interests duty" exists for financial advisers (s961B) AND mortgage brokers (NCCP) as two legally distinct concepts that happen to share a name.
The fix for all of these is the same: read the question twice, identify which trick (if any) is being used, and answer the question that's actually being asked — not the one you assumed was being asked.
